How to Play

Fed Chairman Simulator is a game that puts you in the seat of Fed Chairman. Below are the metrics you'll need to be familiar with in order to sucessfully navtigate your term in office.

You must last your 4 year term (1460 days, or 25 real life minutes) as Fed Chairman while maintaing the reputation of the Federal Reserve.

In order to achieve this it's critical you stick to your mandates as much as possible, which are to keep GDP above $40k per capita and CPI Inflation between the listed targets:



Every day these GDP & CPI targets are met you gain reputation points, and every day they are not met you lose reputation points.

If you lose too many reputation points you'll be removed from office.

Currency per capita refers to how many units of currency exist per person. This number is also the debt per capita, since all currency is created from debt in the fiat system. The higher debt grows the more drag it puts on the economy, since more and more currency is required to service the debt.

Velocity refers to how fast currency moves throughout the economy.

GDP Per Capita is a function of Currency multiplied by Velocity, and is a rough indicator for how well the average person is doing financially.

CPI Inflation refers to how fast consumer prices are rising or falling annually. This is affected by the currency supply, velocity and production.

The Production Index refers to how many physical goods the economy is producing. If it's high the economy is geared more toward producing goods, whereas if it's low the economy is more focused on investing in speculative assets.

Lower production increases CPI Inflation as there are fewer goods to go around, and vice versa higher production tamps down prices.

Low interest rates reduce production as captial flows toward speculative unproductive ventures, whereas higher interest rates deflate speculative bubbles and direct capital toward productive investments.

The two main levers you control are Interest Rates and Quantitative Easing/Tightening. Both of these affect how much currency is in circulation, with higher rates/tighter policy reducing the amount of currency available, and vice versa lower rates increasing the currency available.
